Lasered means getting shot with perfect accuracy — like being hit by a laser beam. No spread, no missed shots, just pure precision cutting through the air. When you get lasered, you know your opponent didn't get lucky — they earned that kill.

## Trajectory & Chronology

'Lasered' entered gaming vocabulary through FPS communities in the early 2010s, initially describing weapons with minimal recoil that fired in a straight line — 'laser beams.' Players would say 'That gun lasers' to mean it had no spread. By the mid-2010s, the term had evolved to describe not just the weapon but the player: 'He lasered me' meant the opponent's aim was so precise it felt like being hit by a laser. The term gained significant popularity with Call of Duty's laser sight attachments and the rise of recoil-control gameplay. By 2020, 'lasered' was standard FPS vocabulary, used across Call of Duty, Apex Legends, and VALORANT. In 2026, 'lasered' remains one of the most commonly used terms to describe precise aim, often appearing in esports commentary and streamer reactions.

## Socio-Cultural Gain

Lasered represents the aesthetic of perfect aim in FPS culture — the visual of a straight line of bullets connecting player to target. Unlike 'sprayed down' (messy, high-volume shooting) or 'one-tapped' (single shot precision), 'lasered' occupies a specific niche: sustained, perfect tracking aim. The term highlights how FPS players categorize different types of aim. A 'flick' is fast and reactive. A 'track' is smooth and sustained. A 'laser' is a track so perfect it looks inhuman. Getting lasered also carries different social weight depending on context. In casual play, it's impressive. In ranked, it's expected at high levels. In esports, being known as a 'laser' (someone who consistently lasers opponents) is a badge of honor — it means your mechanical skill is among the best.

## FAQ

**Q: What's the difference between "lasered" and "one-tapped"?**
> "One-tapped" is a single headshot, instant kill — relying on reaction speed and precision. "Lasered" is multiple bullets hitting consecutively in a straight beam — relying on tracking aim and recoil control. One-tap is the sniper; laser is the rifler. Both demonstrate elite aim but require different technical skills.

**Q: How do I prevent getting lasered?**
> Unpredictable movement is key. Left-right-left-right is predictable; good players will laser you. Use crouch-spamming, jumping, and sudden direction changes to break their tracking. Also, don't peek the same angle twice — they already read your movement pattern the first time.

**Q: Is "lasered" still popular in 2026?**
> Very popular. As one of the core FPS terms, "lasered" has become standard vocabulary for describing precise aim. It appears in SpawnPoint's 2026 glossary and is frequently used in esports commentary. The term describes an eternal gaming phenomenon — perfect aim — so it will never become outdated.

**Q: How do I explain "lasered" to a non-gamer in one sentence?**
