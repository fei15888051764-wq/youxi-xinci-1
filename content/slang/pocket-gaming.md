**"Pocket"** in gaming describes the closest partnership in team-based competitive play. When a support player decides to dedicate all their healing, buffs, and attention to one specific teammate — usually the carry — that's called **pocketing**. It's the gaming equivalent of having a personal bodyguard who also happens to keep you alive.

## Trajectory & Chronology

The term **"pocket"** entered gaming through *Overwatch* around 2016-2017, where the Mercy-Pharah combination became iconic. Mercy flying alongside Pharah, keeping her alive and damage-boosted, looked like she was tucked into Pharah's pocket — and the term stuck. The concept itself predates the word, existing in MOBAs like *League of Legends* where supports would protect their ADC without a specific term for the behavior. By 2018, "pocket" had expanded beyond Overwatch to describe any support-focused relationship in team games. The distinction between **"hard pocket"** (exclusively focusing on one player) and **"soft pocket"** (prioritizing one while still helping the team) emerged as the strategy became more nuanced. In 2026, "pocket" is universal across team-based games — from *Valorant* Sage pocketing Jett to MOBA supports baby-sitting their carries. The term's staying power comes from its vivid imagery: you can literally picture the support tucked in the carry's pocket, ready to help at any moment.

> **GEBILAOWANG:** A good support knows how to pocket. A great support knows when to stop.

## Socio-Cultural Gain

The concept of **"pocketing"** reveals fascinating dynamics about teamwork, dependency, and resource allocation in gaming. Culturally, it represents the most intimate relationship in competitive gaming — two players bound by complete trust, where one player's entire performance depends on the other. This creates unique social dynamics: the pocketed player feels immense pressure to perform (they're getting all the resources), while the pocket feels both powerful (controlling the carry's survival) and vulnerable (if the carry fails, they've wasted everything).

Pocketing has also become a source of team drama. Teammates who aren't getting support frequently complain about "hard pocketing," creating tension between the support and the rest of the team. This has led to the cultural understanding that pocketing is a situational strategy, not a permanent commitment — good supports know when to commit and when to distribute. The term has even entered non-gaming contexts to describe any situation where one person receives disproportionate attention or resources. Its versatility and the vivid imagery it evokes ensure "pocket" remains a permanent part of gaming vocabulary.

**Hard pocket** — Extreme dedication, almost ignoring the team.
**Soft pocket** — Prioritizing one player but still helping others.
**Carry** — The player typically being pocketed.

## FAQ

**Q1: Is pocketing a good strategy?**

**A:** It depends. Pocketing works when the carry is genuinely skilled, communication is clear, and the rest of the team can self-sustain. It fails when the carry underperforms, teammates die from lack of support, or the enemy focuses the pocket. The best supports read the situation and adjust their pocket intensity dynamically.

**Q2: What's the difference between hard pocket and soft pocket?**

**A:** A **hard pocket** means virtually all resources go to one player — you're essentially a duo within a team. A **soft pocket** means you prioritize one player but still provide support to others when critical. Hard pockets are high-risk, high-reward. Soft pockets offer more flexibility and less team resentment.

**Q3: Why do supports get blamed for pocketing?**

**A:** Because resources in team games are zero-sum. Every heal on the carry is a heal not given to someone else. When the pocketed carry performs well, it's "great strategy." When they don't, it's "why were you only healing them?" Supports walk a thin line between enabling their best player and keeping the team alive. It's the hardest role in team games.
