## Origin & History

By 2019, eDPI was standard vocabulary in CS and Valorant aim communities. The concept is simple math: your mouse DPI multiplied by your in-game sensitivity. 800 DPI times 0.5 sens equals 400 eDPI. Done.

It exists because gamers kept comparing settings like idiots. "I play 2.0 sens" means nothing when your mouse is 1600 DPI and mine is 400. Where did eDPI come from? Aim communities needed one number that combined hardware and software, so they made one. Pro settings databases spread it, and now every "what sens do you play" thread expects an eDPI answer.

## Pop Culture Impact

eDPI turned aim settings from superstition into science. Before it, sens talk was pure chaos. Now there's a shared language across every FPS — Valorant pros average around 280, CS2 pros around 800, and everyone can compare instantly.

Why do gamers say eDPI instead of just "sens"? Because it's the only version of the number that survives a hardware change. New mouse, same eDPI, same aim. It also spawned the eternal low-sens versus high-sens war, which will never end and honestly never should.

## FAQ

**Q: Is eDPI the same as cm/360?**
A: Same goal, different format. eDPI is a quick multiplication for comparing inside one game. cm/360 measures physical mouse distance for a full turn, which works across different games. Nerds use cm/360, everyone else uses eDPI.

**Q: What eDPI should I use?**
A: Start in the pro range for your game — roughly 200-400 for Valorant, 400-1200 for CS2 — then adjust by feel. Lower gives control, higher gives speed. Most players are too high, tbh.

**Q: Is eDPI still relevant in 2026?**
A: More than ever. Every new tac shooter launches with a thousand "convert my sens" threads, and eDPI is how those conversations work. The eDPI meaning in gaming is basically permanent infrastructure now.
