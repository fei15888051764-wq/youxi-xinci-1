Cooldown is the wait time after you use an ability before you can use it again. That's it. That's the whole thing.

## The Rise of Cooldown

The word started in gyms, not games. "Cooldown" as the recovery period after exercise has been in print for over a century. Games borrowed it and never gave it back.

The MMO era turned it into core vocabulary. World of Warcraft players lived and died by cooldown timers, with some skills locked for seconds and big ones benched for minutes. League of Legends later added Ability Haste to shave the wait, and even grew sub-species: static cooldowns that can't be reduced, on-target cooldowns per enemy, recharges that stack up charges. Somewhere along the way, "it's on cooldown" became a sentence people say about things that have nothing to do with games.

## How Cooldown Took Over Gaming

From MMO raids to MOBAs to hero shooters. Overwatch locks abilities for six to eight seconds, and entire teamfights hinge on tracking who doesn't have theirs. Mobile games put cooldowns on everything, including, somehow, your stamina.

The term escaped the screen completely. Rest after leg day? Cooldown. Group chat needs a breather after an argument? Cooldown. It's genuinely useful vocabulary. Games just got there first.

## FAQ

**Is cooldown the same as cast time?**

No. Cast time is the wind-up before the ability fires. Cooldown is the recovery after it does. The full sandwich is: cast, effect, cooldown. You wait through the first to use the thing, then through the last to use it again.

**What does "on CD" mean?**

The ability is recharging. When your teammate calls "ult on CD," they're telling you not to ask for it, because it literally cannot happen. There's also the GCD, the global cooldown, a short shared timer stopping you from firing everything at once.

**How do I explain cooldown to someone who doesn't game?**

"It's like a microwave. Press the button, it runs, it dings, and only then can you press it again. Except the button is a fireball." Works every time.
