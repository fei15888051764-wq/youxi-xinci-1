In gaming, **BM** stands for **Bad Manners** — the art of being a poor winner or a sore loser.

---

---

## Trajectory & Chronology

The term 'BM' (Bad Manners) originated in traditional sports and card games before transitioning into gaming culture in the early 2000s. StarCraft's Korean professional scene in the early 2000s famously categorized player behavior, making BM a recognized term in esports. As online gaming grew, BM evolved from simple trash talk to include teabagging, emote spamming, and intentionally prolonging games to humiliate opponents. In 2026, most competitive games have reporting systems specifically for BM behavior.

## Socio-Cultural Gain

BM occupies a complex space in gaming culture. While genuinely toxic behavior is universally condemned, lighthearted BM has become part of competitive gaming's entertainment value. Streamers and pros often use controlled BM to entertain audiences, creating 'heel' personas that fans love to hate. The line between acceptable banter and toxic behavior remains debated, with different gaming communities having varying tolerance levels. In 2026, most esports organizations have conduct guidelines that address BM, recognizing its impact on both player mental health and audience perception.

## FAQ

**Q1: Is BM the same as being toxic?**

Not exactly. BM is usually intentional and performative (taunting, emotes, etc.). Toxicity is more about genuine negativity and harassment. Lighthearted BM can be part of competitive fun; toxicity never is.

**Q2: Can I get banned for BM?**

Depends on severity. Emote spamming or light taunting usually won't get you banned. Prolonging games, harassment, or abusive language can result in reports and penalties.

**Q3: Is BM more common in certain games?**

Yes. Fighting games and MOBAs have a reputation for BM due to their 1v1 nature and long match times. Card games like Hearthstone also see a lot of emote-based BM.
