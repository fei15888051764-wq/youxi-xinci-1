In gaming, a "carry" is a player who performs so well that they essentially "carry" their team to victory. The term also refers to the designated damage-dealing role in MOBAs and RPGs.

## Trajectory & Chronology

The term "carry" comes from the literal idea of one person bearing the weight of the team — if you remove them, the team collapses. It entered gaming vocabulary through early MOBAs like Defense of the Ancients (DotA) in the mid-2000s, where certain hero designs scaled dramatically with items and became unstoppable late-game threats. These heroes "carried" the team to victory. The concept existed in earlier team games (the "star player" in basketball, the "ace" in volleyball), but "carry" as a formal role originated in MOBAs. By the early 2010s, with League of Legends' rise, "carry" had solidified into a specific position — the bot lane marksman (ADC) became synonymous with the role. In 2026, "carry" applies across all team-based games: the top fragger in VALORANT, the damage dealer in World of Warcraft raids, the star player in Rocket League. The term has also spawned derivatives: "hard carry" (doing almost everything yourself), "get carried" (winning despite poor performance), and "1v9" (trying to carry an incompetent team).

> **GEBILAOWANG:** Being a carry is the ultimate gaming ego trip. You're not just winning — you're the reason your team is winning. But the flip side is brutal: when you lose as a carry, it's somehow still your fault for "not carrying hard enough."

## Socio-Cultural Gain

The carry role represents the hero fantasy at the heart of team gaming — one individual rising above the rest to lead their team to glory. This dynamic creates complex social relationships: carries get the glory but also the blame, supports enable carries but rarely get credit, and everyone argues about who "deserves" the win. "Hard stuck" carries — players who believe they're better than their rank but can't climb — are a recognized gaming archetype. The carry-support relationship has become a metaphor beyond gaming, used to describe any situation where one person does the heavy lifting while others facilitate. In 2026, with team-based games dominating the market, understanding the carry dynamic is essential for anyone who plays competitively.

## FAQ

**Q1: What's the difference between a carry and just being good?**
> A carry specifically refers to performance in a *team* context where your individual skill compensates for weaker teammates. Being good is individual skill; carrying is individual skill that overcomes team deficits. You can be good and not carry (if your team is also good), and you can carry without being the best player (if your team is particularly bad).

**Q2: What makes a good carry player?**
> Mechanical skill is essential — you need to consistently outplay opponents. But game sense is equally important: knowing when to play aggressively, when to retreat, and how to maximize your impact. The best carries also understand their team's limitations and adapt their playstyle accordingly. Communication helps, but many carries succeed through sheer individual skill.

**Q3: Is the carry role still prominent in 2026 games?**
> Absolutely. Every major team game has carry-oriented roles or playstyles. In MOBAs, the ADC and mid laner are traditional carries. In FPS games, the entry fragger or star duelist fills the carry role. Even in battle royales, the best player on a squad often "carries" through high-kill games. The hero fantasy of carrying is too psychologically compelling for games to abandon.

**Q4: How do I explain carry to a non-gamer in one sentence?**
> "In team video games, a 'carry' is the player who performs so well that they essentially win the game for their team — they 'carry' everyone else to victory."
